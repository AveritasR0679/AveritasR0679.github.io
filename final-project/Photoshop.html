Raster and Vector

Vectors:

They are comprised of paths, which are defined by a start and end point, along with other points, curves, and angles along the way.

Bitmaps:

Bitmaps represent a grid of pixels. Most images you see on your computer screen are raster graphics.

Black and White

Masks and Channels

Masks isolate and protect parts of an image. When you create a mask based on a selection, the area you haven't selected is masked, or protected from editing. With masks you can create and save time-consuming selections and use them again. In addition, you can use masks for other complex editing tasks - applying color change and filters.

Mask Types

In Phososhop, you can make temporary masks, called quick masks, or you can create permanent masks and store them as special greyscale channels called alpha channels. You can also manipulate channels in the Channel Mixer which contains a bunch of pre-defined basic filters; by all means, you can add your own custom channel with a unique set of parameters as well.

Smart Objects

What are smart objects?

Smart objects enable you to make unlimited changes without losing quality and provide an amazing amount of flexibility. To put it simply, you can think of a smart object as a bag When you convert a raster into a smart object, you are basically putting your image into a "bag." No matter how you alter the "bag," the stuff inside it will not be changed. In other words, you are able to activate and deactivate the changes and adjustments you've made.

How to convert regular bitmaps to smart objects?

Right click on the target layer and select "convert to Smart Object". You can edit the smart object by directly applying the filter, which will not alter the original image. If you wish to edit the original file, you can double click on the smart object icon, which will open another Photoshop window where you can make all the necessary changes. After you're done, save the file. The smart object will be automatically updated.

Comments:

Smart objects are extremely easy to use when you are dealing with multiple instances of the object. When you make changes to the image within the smart object, the many instances of this object will be updated altogether without you manually editing them one by one.

Retouching Photos

Tools involved in retouching photos:

  • Straighten and crop an image
  • Adjust the tonal range of an image
  • Use the Spot Healing Brush tool to repair part of an image/li>
  • Use the content-aware patch tool to remove or replace objects
  • Use the Clone Stamp tol to touch up areas
  • Remove digital artifacts from an image
  • Apply the Smart Sharpen filter to finish retouching photos

Strategies for retouching

How much retouching you do depends on the image yo're working on and your goals for it. For many images, you may need only to change the resolution, lighten the image, or repair a minor blemish. For others, you may need to perform several tasks and employ more advanced tools and techniques.

Comments:

A tip for retouching photos. Taking snapshots plays a pivotal role in retouching photos for two reasons. First, a snapshot can serve as a baseline for any future edits. If you are unsatisfied with the result, you can just simply go back to the snapshot you've taken without doing extra work to undo your steps. Second, you are able to switch back and forth between different snapshots you've taken to compare the original image with the edited one.
